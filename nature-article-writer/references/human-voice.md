# Human-Sounding Scientific Prose

`Human-sounding` in a Nature-style paper means **precise, calm, field-aware, and non-generic**. It does **not** mean chatty, confessional, or literary.

This file adapts the anti-slop ideas from the bundled archive resources to scientific writing rather than general prose.

## What makes AI-ish scientific writing obvious

### 1. Inflated significance sentences

Weak:
- `These findings highlight the critical importance of...`
- `Our work paves the way for future studies...`
- `This study provides novel insights into...`

Better:
- state the exact implication
- say what the result changes in the field
- keep the scope proportional

### 2. Generic abstract nouns

Watch for:
- landscape
- interplay
- framework
- paradigm
- leveraging
- fostering
- tapestry
- avenue
- underscore
- highlight

These words are not always wrong, but they often signal that the prose has drifted away from concrete evidence.

### 3. Transition-word conveyor belt

Watch for repeated paragraph openings such as:
- Additionally
- Moreover
- Importantly
- Notably
- Overall
- Taken together
- In this context

Use them only when they genuinely clarify structure.

### 4. Formulaic contrast frames

Watch for:
- not only...but also
- not X but Y
- not merely
- rather than simply
- while this is important, it also...

These frames can sound rehearsed when overused. State the claim directly.

### 5. Hype adjectives

Watch for:
- novel
- groundbreaking
- remarkable
- unprecedented
- transformative
- robust
- elegant
- compelling

If the result is strong, the data can carry that weight.

### 6. Vague agency

Weak:
- `The field is increasingly recognising...`
- `The data tell us...`
- `The conversation has shifted...`

Better:
- name the authors, experiments, or cited work
- say what the analysis supports
- make agency and evidence legible

### 7. Mechanical rhythm

A paragraph where every sentence has the same length and shape feels generated. Vary length modestly, but do not become ornate.

### 8. Empty endings

Weak endings:
- `These findings open new avenues for future studies.`
- `Further work will be needed to fully understand...`
- `This work may have broad implications across biology.`

Better:
- state the concrete next implication
- state the limitation
- end with the strongest defensible consequence of the result

## Better defaults

Prefer:
- concrete verbs over abstract nouns
- one main claim per sentence
- explicit nouns over vague pronouns
- short sentences after dense ones
- active voice when it clarifies agency
- calibrated uncertainty where appropriate

Examples:
- `We measured transcriptional variability across 12 donors.`
- `The model predicted response only in tumours with high baseline interferon signalling.`
- `These data are consistent with a transport bottleneck rather than a differentiation defect.`

## Scientific prose rewrites

### Example 1 — vague importance

Before:
> Importantly, these findings underscore the critical role of mitochondrial quality control in shaping the broader ageing landscape.

After:
> The data indicate that impaired mitochondrial quality control contributes to the ageing phenotype in this model.

### Example 2 — generic novelty claim

Before:
> Taken together, our study provides novel insights into tumour evolution and establishes a robust framework for future translational investigations.

After:
> Our results show how the tumour changes across treatment and identify two features that can now be tested in prospective cohorts.

### Example 3 — abstract noun overload

Before:
> This work reveals a complex interplay between chromatin remodelling and lineage commitment in the developmental landscape.

After:
> The results link chromatin remodelling to the timing of lineage commitment during development.

### Example 4 — rhetorical contrast

Before:
> This is not merely a descriptive atlas, but a transformative resource that fundamentally changes how the field will approach cell-state annotation.

After:
> The atlas resolves cell states that earlier datasets merged and provides a reference for annotation in related tissues.

### Example 5 — empty closing sentence

Before:
> These findings open exciting avenues for future studies of disease progression.

After:
> The same markers should now be tested in longitudinal cohorts to determine whether they predict progression before symptoms appear.

## Phrase bank to cut or replace

Cut aggressively:
- It is important to note that
- In the broader context
- Taken together
- Overall, these data
- This study sought to
- plays a crucial role
- highlights the importance of
- provides a basis for
- opens new avenues
- sheds light on
- state-of-the-art
- robust framework
- significant implications

Prefer replacements such as:
- the exact implication
- the exact mechanism
- the exact limitation
- the exact next test

## Do not over-correct

Do **not** make scientific prose sound casual.

Avoid:
- jokes
- rhetorical questions
- dramatic fragments
- first-person opinions about feelings
- forced punchiness
- slangy contractions

A Nature-style paper should sound written by a careful scientist, not by a marketing team and not by a social-media humanizer.

## Fast checks

- Could this sentence appear in almost any paper? If yes, rewrite it.
- Is the noun doing too much work? Replace with a verb.
- Is the adjective carrying the argument? Replace with evidence.
- Is the paragraph ending in a cliché? Replace it with the concrete implication or limitation.
- Did you use `Here we show` once and then move on? Good. Do not repeat it throughout the paper.
